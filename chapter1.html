Chapter 01

Tech's Impact on Business & Society

Why technology is the most important force reshaping business today — and why you need to understand it

📖
Vocabulary
Market Capitalization (Market Cap)
The total market value of a publicly traded company, calculated by multiplying the number of shares outstanding by the current share price. Market cap is the most commonly used measure of a company's size and value in financial markets. As of this writing, only six firms have ever surpassed $1 trillion in market cap — Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet (Google), Meta, Amazon, and Nvidia — and all are technology companies.
🧠 Analogy: If a company were a house, market cap is what the market thinks the house is worth right now — not what you paid for it, but what someone would pay for it today.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1; Investopedia.com/market-capitalization
Digital Transformation
The process by which firms integrate digital technology into all areas of their business, fundamentally changing how they operate and deliver value to customers. It's not just adding technology — it's rethinking business models, processes, and culture around digital capabilities. A Forbes report found that 72% of firms are currently failing at digital transformation, highlighting how difficult it is and how urgently capable managers are needed.
🧠 Analogy: Digital transformation isn't like adding a new room to your house. It's more like demolishing the house and rebuilding it from scratch around electricity, internet, and smart systems that didn't exist when the original was built.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1; Forbes
Platform (vs. Product)
A platform is a product or service that allows others to build complementary goods and services on top of it, creating an ecosystem of value beyond what the platform creator alone could build. A product is a standalone offering with fixed features. Apple's key insight was to build a platform (iOS + App Store) rather than just a product (a phone) — enabling 2+ million apps built by third parties that made the iPhone exponentially more valuable than Apple could have made it alone.
🧠 Analogy: A product is a shopping mall store — it sells what it sells. A platform is the mall itself — it provides infrastructure and attracts dozens of stores, restaurants, and services that make the mall far more valuable than any one tenant.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
Technology that enables computers to perform tasks that would normally require human intelligence — understanding language, recognizing images, making decisions, generating content, and learning from data. Modern AI is powered by large language models (LLMs) trained on massive datasets. Bill Gates has called AI "the most transformative technology any of us will see in our lifetimes" — ahead of the PC, internet, and smartphones.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1
Generative AI (GenAI)
A category of AI that can generate new content — text, images, code, audio, and video — based on prompts from users. ChatGPT (OpenAI), Gemini (Google), and Copilot (Microsoft) are leading examples. GenAI reached 100 million users in just 2 months — faster than any technology in history. It took telephones 75 years to reach 50 million users. GenAI is also prone to "hallucination" — confidently generating false information that sounds plausible.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1
Server Farm
A massive collection of computer servers running software to coordinate their collective use, providing the infrastructure backbone for cloud computing, AI, SaaS, and large-scale internet services. AI requires enormous server farms loaded with specialized chips (primarily from Nvidia). Ireland's data centers alone now consume 18% of the country's electricity — illustrating the massive energy demands of modern computing infrastructure.
🧠 Analogy: A server farm is like a massive power plant — except instead of generating electricity, it generates computing power that gets delivered over the internet to anyone who needs it.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1
R&D (Research and Development)
The portion of a firm focused on innovation — developing new products, technologies, and capabilities that will drive future growth. All six trillion-dollar tech firms are investing the largest portions of their R&D budgets into artificial intelligence, signaling where competitive advantage will come from in the next decade. R&D spending is a leading indicator of where an industry is heading.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1
Network Effects
A phenomenon where a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. Meta has 3.6 billion monthly users — every additional user makes the platform more useful for everyone else because there are more people to connect with. Network effects create powerful winner-take-all dynamics: the platform with the most users attracts even more users, making it nearly impossible for competitors to catch up once a leader is established.
🧠 Analogy: A phone is useless if you're the only one who has one. The more people who have phones, the more valuable your phone becomes. That's a network effect.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1; Ch. 3
Disruptive Technology
A technology that fundamentally transforms an industry, often displacing established firms and creating entirely new markets. Uber disrupted taxis without owning vehicles. Airbnb disrupted hotels without owning properties. TikTok disrupted social media when everyone thought the battle was between Instagram and Snapchat. Disruption almost always comes from outside the existing industry — incumbents rarely disrupt themselves.
🧠 Analogy: Disruption is like a new road being built that bypasses an entire town. The town's businesses didn't do anything wrong — but traffic stopped coming because a faster route appeared somewhere else.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 1

Key Concepts
The Six Trillion-Dollar Tech Firms — What They Actually Do
Understanding what makes each firm dominant is more important than memorizing their names. Notice that each originally dominated one market — but they're increasingly competing with each other.
Apple
Consumer hardware + software ecosystem. First to $1T. Pivoted from products to platforms. Services (Music, TV+, iCloud) now bigger than Boeing, Nike, or Oracle.
Microsoft
Enterprise software (Windows, Office) + Azure cloud. Invested $13B in OpenAI/ChatGPT. Integrating AI (Copilot) into every product.
Alphabet (Google)
Advertising + search. Most profitable media company ever. Divisions in driverless cars, life extension, cloud, and AI. CEO called AI as profound as electricity.
Meta
Social media (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp). 3.6 billion monthly users — no government has ever reached that scale. Lost $46B+ on the Metaverse bet.
Amazon
E-commerce + AWS cloud. AWS is more profitable than the entire retail business. Started selling books. Now runs the internet infrastructure for thousands of firms.
Nvidia
The "arms merchant" of AI — makes the chips everyone else uses. Controls 80–95% of the high-end AI chip market. Doesn't build AI products, it powers all of them.
AI: Promise and Peril — Know Both Sides
This is heavily tested because the textbook explicitly covers both — and your professor wants you to think critically, not just cheerleadfor tech.
✅ Promise
• Accelerated COVID vaccine development — saved millions of lives
• Lawyers automating tedious case research
• Accountants eliminating grunt work, focusing on high-value analysis
• Diagnosing diseases earlier and more accurately
• Fighting crime, advancing clean energy
• Delivering medical supplies to remote areas (Zipline drones)
• Empowering programmers to build faster
⚠️ Peril
• Hallucination — AI confidently generates false information
• Biased systems harming women and people of color
• Copyright violations — trained on artists' and writers' work without consent
• Security holes from unvetted consumer AI tools used in enterprises
• Enabling scammers, hackers, and bad actors
• Massive energy consumption (Ireland data centers = 18% of national electricity)
• Job displacement uncertainty — nobody knows the full scale yet
The "Platform, Not Product" Insight
The biggest strategic lesson of this chapter is Apple's insight: build a platform, not just a product.

A phone is a product — it does what it does. But iOS + the App Store turned the iPhone into a platform where millions of third-party developers could build apps, creating value Apple could never have generated alone. 50,000 apps appeared in under a year. Today there are 2+ million.

The result? Network effects kicked in: more apps attracted more users, more users attracted more developers, more developers built more apps. The cycle became self-reinforcing and nearly impossible for competitors to break.

Apple's services business (Music, TV+, Arcade, iCloud, News, Fitness) — built entirely on the platform they created — now generates more revenue than Boeing, Coke, Nike, Oracle, or Delta Airlines. That's what platforms do that products can't.
Tech Disruption Is Unpredictable — And That's the Point
The textbook uses several examples to make one key managerial point: the next disruptor almost always comes from somewhere nobody expected.

• Uber owns no vehicles. Airbnb owns no hotels. Facebook creates no content. Alibaba holds no inventory. Yet all dominate their industries.
• Before smartphones, parents said "never get in a stranger's car." Now we use smartphones to summon strangers into our cars.
• Everyone thought the social media battle was Instagram vs. Snapchat. TikTok came from China and became a generation's primary media platform.
• Web 3.0 was "the next big thing" — it wasn't. Zuckerberg lost $46B+ betting on the Metaverse.

The managerial lesson: don't assume your industry's competitive map is stable. Half the Fortune 500 companies from 2000 have fallen off the list since then — through mergers, acquisitions, or bankruptcies caused by tech disruption. The threat always arrives from outside.
Tech in Emerging Markets — The Democratization of Computing
One of the chapter's most important and underappreciated points: technology is not just a rich-country phenomenon.

70% of sub-Saharan Africa's population lives within range of mobile coverage — more than those with access to clean water or electricity. Fast/cheap tech is becoming an economic engine for the world's poorest populations:

• Esoko (Ghana) — delivers commodity prices and farming tips via text message to rural farmers, raising incomes and protecting them from being cheated by middlemen who once exploited their lack of market information
• M-Pesa (Kenya) — mobile banking via text message. 25 million users vs. only 4 million with traditional bank accounts. 44% of Kenya's GDP flows through the service
• Afghan Police Mobile Pay — when officers switched to receiving pay via mobile money, many thought they'd gotten a raise — because the middlemen who had been skimming their cash salaries could no longer steal from them

Economist Jeffrey Sachs called the cell phone "the single most transformative technology for world economic development." Moore's Law enabling sub-$20 smartphones made this possible.
Your Career Will Be Defined by Tech — Whether You're "Technical" or Not
This chapter opens with a direct message to you as a student: tech is not the future — it's now, and it will reshape every job you ever have.

When spreadsheets arrived, accountants didn't disappear — the role expanded and became more valuable because the boring math was automated. When word processors arrived, secretaries didn't disappear — they did more. The same pattern is happening now with AI.

The World Economic Forum's "Jobs of Tomorrow" report:
• Data and AI jobs growing 37%
• Engineering and cloud computing jobs growing 34%
• Business Insider ranks software developer as the top job for the next decade

The key insight: past tech shifts required MORE workers with higher technical skills, not fewer. Even non-tech jobs — HR, marketing, advertising — are now data-driven. You don't have to be a programmer, but you must understand technology well enough to make decisions about it, evaluate vendors, manage technical teams, and spot opportunities before your competitors do.

Question 4
When Afghan police officers began receiving their pay via mobile money (M-Pesa style), many thought they had received a pay raise. They hadn't. What actually happened?
A
The government increased police salaries as part of a digital transformation initiative.
B
Mobile money automatically calculated overtime pay that paper systems had missed.
C
The middlemen distributing cash salaries had been skimming money from each officer's pay. Digital payment eliminated the middleman and stopped the theft.
D
Currency exchange rates improved when salaries were converted to digital format.
✓ Correct! This is one of the most powerful examples in the chapter because it shows technology solving a human corruption problem. When cash changes hands through multiple layers of bureaucracy, skimming is easy and nearly undetectable. Digital payments create a transparent, auditable record that makes theft instantly visible — effectively eliminating the opportunity for corruption. Technology didn't just deliver pay more efficiently; it changed the power dynamic entirely.
✗ Not quite. The officers' pay hadn't changed at all — what changed was that the people distributing their cash had previously been stealing a portion of each paycheck. Mobile money sends funds directly to each officer, cutting out the corrupt middlemen entirely. The "raise" was just receiving what they were always supposed to have been paid. This is the textbook's example of how technology can cut corruption.
