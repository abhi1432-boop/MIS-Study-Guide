Chapter 09

Cloud Computing, SaaS & AWS

How renting computing changed every industry — and why Amazon runs the internet

📖
Vocabulary
Cloud Computing
Replacing computing resources — either an organization's hardware or software — with services provided over the internet. Instead of buying and maintaining your own servers and software, you rent what you need from a provider like Amazon (AWS), Microsoft (Azure), or Google (GCP) and pay only for what you use, like a utility bill. The "cloud" is just someone else's computer — a massive server farm somewhere you never see.
🧠 Analogy: Cloud computing is like the electrical grid. You don't build your own power plant — you plug in and pay for the kilowatts you use. AWS is the power plant.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Software as a Service (SaaS)
A cloud model where a third-party vendor hosts software on their own servers and delivers it to users over the internet — usually through a web browser. The customer never installs, maintains, or updates the software. They simply pay a subscription fee and log in. Examples: Salesforce (CRM), Google Docs, Slack, Zoom, Adobe Creative Cloud. SaaS handles everything: software, hardware, security, backups, and upgrades.
🧠 Analogy: SaaS is Netflix instead of buying DVDs. You don't own anything — you subscribe, stream, and always get the latest version with zero maintenance.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9; Salesforce.com
Platform as a Service (PaaS)
A cloud model where the vendor provides the platform — operating system, database, development tools, testing environment, and hosting hardware — but the client writes and deploys their own custom software on top of it. You bring the code; they handle the infrastructure. Examples: Google App Engine, AWS Elastic Beanstalk, Salesforce Force.com. PaaS requires more management than SaaS but gives developers more control.
🧠 Analogy: PaaS is renting a fully equipped commercial kitchen. The stoves, ovens, and equipment are provided — you just bring your own recipes and cook.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)
A cloud model where the vendor provides only the raw hardware — computing power, storage, and networking — over the internet. The client is responsible for everything else: choosing the OS, installing databases, deploying apps, and managing security. Maximum control, maximum responsibility. Examples: Amazon EC2, Microsoft Azure VMs, Google Compute Engine. IaaS is the "blank canvas" of cloud — ideal for firms with very specific technical needs.
🧠 Analogy: IaaS is renting an empty warehouse. You get the building and the electricity — you bring all the furniture, equipment, and staff yourself.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Amazon Web Services (AWS)
Amazon's cloud computing division — the world's largest cloud provider, controlling roughly one-third of the global cloud market. Launched in 2006, AWS offers hundreds of services including computing (EC2, Lambda), storage (S3), databases (Redshift, DynamoDB), and AI tools. AWS generated over $80 billion in annual revenue (2022), which is more than Nike or Coca-Cola, and is responsible for the majority of Amazon's operating profit — more profitable than the entire retail business.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9; Amazon Annual Report 2022
Service Level Agreement (SLA)
A negotiated contract between a customer and a vendor that defines the minimum standards for service — typically covering uptime guarantees, performance benchmarks, and response times for resolving issues. For SaaS, SLAs usually guarantee a specific percentage of system availability (e.g., "99.9% uptime"). If the vendor fails to meet the SLA, the customer may be entitled to credits or other remedies. SLAs are the primary legal protection customers have against cloud outages.
🧠 Analogy: An SLA is like a landlord promising your apartment will always have heat and hot water. If it goes out for more than X hours, you're owed a rent reduction.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Virtualization
Software that allows a single physical computer to behave as many separate virtual machines, each running its own OS and applications independently. This is the core technology that makes cloud computing possible — AWS doesn't dedicate one server per customer; it runs dozens of virtual machines on each physical server, charging each customer only for their share. Virtualization also dramatically improves hardware utilization — from typical 15% capacity to 80%+.
🧠 Analogy: Virtualization is like converting one house into 20 apartments. The building (hardware) is the same — but now it serves 20 independent tenants (virtual machines) instead of one.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Cloudbursting
A hybrid cloud strategy where a firm's private data center handles normal workloads, but automatically spills over to a public cloud provider during periods of peak demand. The public cloud acts as an overflow valve — you only pay for the extra capacity when you actually need it. This is especially valuable for handling unpredictable demand spikes or "black swan" events (rare, high-impact surges in traffic).
🧠 Analogy: Your home has a water heater (private cloud) for daily use. When 30 guests show up for a party, you hook up an extra tank (public cloud burst) just for that day — then disconnect it when everyone leaves.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Consumerization of Technology
The trend where employees independently adopt consumer-grade cloud tools (Dropbox, Slack, ChatGPT, Google Docs) for work purposes, without IT department approval or oversight. This can drive creativity and cost savings, but also creates security risks, legal concerns, and operational inconsistencies — especially when sensitive corporate data ends up in unauthorized third-party systems. Requires deeper collaboration between IT and business teams.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9
Thin Client / Thin Device
A computing device with minimal local processing power that relies on a remote server (in the cloud) to perform most or all computing tasks. Amazon's Echo, Fire TV stick, and Kindle are all thin devices — the "brains" live in AWS data centers, not in the device itself. This keeps the physical device cheap and simple while offloading complex tasks (voice recognition, recommendations, media storage) to powerful remote servers.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 9

Key Concepts
SaaS vs. PaaS vs. IaaS — The Cloud Service Stack
The key question in cloud computing is: how much do you want the vendor to handle vs. how much control do you want? The more control you want, the more you manage yourself.
☁️ SaaS — Software as a Service
Vendor handles everything: software, hardware, security, backups, upgrades. You just log in.
Who uses it: Any firm wanting off-the-shelf software with zero IT burden. → Salesforce, Google Docs, Zoom, Adobe CC
🔧 PaaS — Platform as a Service
Vendor provides OS, database, dev tools, and hosting. You write and deploy your own custom code on top.
Who uses it: Dev teams building custom apps who don't want to manage infrastructure. → Google App Engine, Salesforce Force.com
⚙️ IaaS — Infrastructure as a Service
Vendor provides raw hardware only. You choose OS, software, databases, and manage everything else yourself.
Who uses it: Technical teams needing maximum flexibility and control. → Amazon EC2, Microsoft Azure VMs
Rule of thumb: SaaS = least control, least IT work. IaaS = most control, most IT work. Most large firms use all three for different applications.
Why Firms Choose SaaS: Benefits vs. Risks
Forrester Research estimates SaaS can bring cost savings of 25–60% when all factors are included. Here's the full picture:
✅ Lower Costs
No upfront hardware, software licenses, or IT staff to run it. Costs shift from large capital expenses to flexible monthly subscriptions.
⚠️ Vendor Dependence
If your SaaS vendor goes bankrupt, you're stuck. They have your data, and you may have no way to quickly absorb that function in-house.
✅ Scalability
Need more capacity for holiday season? The vendor handles it. You never have to buy servers for worst-case scenarios you'll only hit once a year.
⚠️ Internet Dependency
If your internet goes down, access to your SaaS system goes down too. Everything is only as reliable as your connection.
✅ Always Up to Date
Vendor instantly pushes bug fixes and upgrades to all customers — no IT project required. You always use the latest version.
⚠️ Forced Upgrades
You can't stay on an old version. When the vendor updates the UI or workflow, your staff may need retraining whether you want it or not.
✅ Variable Cost
Pay $75/user/month — shrink during slow seasons, scale up when you add staff. Far less financial risk than a $15M ERP installation.
⚠️ Data & Security Risk
Your data lives on someone else's servers. Regulatory issues, international data laws, and security breaches are all out of your direct control.
How SaaS Vendors Make Money — and Why They Choose This Model
Revenue model: Most SaaS firms charge a monthly or annual subscription per user (e.g., Salesforce at ~$75/user/month). Others offer free tiers supported by advertising, or charge more for premium features.

Why vendors prefer SaaS over traditional licensing:
• One version only: A packaged software firm (like SAP) must maintain multiple versions for Windows, Mac, Linux. A SaaS firm develops, tests, and supports exactly one version — on their own hardware. Far simpler.
• Tighter feedback loop: Since the vendor runs your software on their servers, they can see how it's being used, where it fails, and what needs improvement — accelerating product iteration.
• No piracy: You can't illegally copy a subscription service that runs on someone else's hardware.
• Direct distribution: No retail stores or middlemen. Available globally to anyone with a browser from day one — including niche "vertical market" customers (legal firms, medical practices) that a physical distribution model could never reach cost-effectively.
Cloud Computing Lowers Barriers to Entry — Disrupting Every Industry
Before cloud computing, launching a tech startup required millions of dollars just in hardware and software infrastructure before you could write a single line of your actual product. AWS changed that completely.

Venture capitalist Mark Suster estimates the cost to start an internet company fell from $5 million in 2000 to just $5,000 in 2011 — largely due to cloud computing and OSS. The CEO of payments startup Zuora: "We have no servers. We run the entire business in the cloud." Enterprise firm Apttus did six months of development in a couple of weekends using cloud services.

Effect on new entrants: Startups can launch, scale, and compete with giants from day one. The technical infrastructure advantage incumbents once held has largely disappeared.

Effect on existing competitors: Every established firm in every industry now faces disruption from startups with near-zero infrastructure costs. The moat of "we can afford servers and they can't" no longer exists. This intensifies competition and demands that incumbents innovate faster.
AWS: Why Amazon Dominates the Cloud
AWS launched in 2006 — Google's competitor wasn't available until 2008, Microsoft's Azure until 2010. That 4–7 year head start created compounding advantages:

• Market share: AWS controls ~32% of global cloud market — roughly equal to the next two competitors (Microsoft Azure at 22%, Google at 11%) combined.
• Revenue: $80+ billion annually (2022), more than United Airlines, Nike, or Coca-Cola.
• Lock-in: Once a firm builds its systems on AWS services (EC2, S3, Lambda, etc.), migrating to a competitor is extremely costly and time-consuming.
• Network effects: Millions of AWS-trained developers, thousands of third-party tools built on AWS, and a vast ecosystem of compatible software all reinforce each other.

Even Amazon's direct competitors (Netflix, Salesforce, Dropbox) use AWS — because its infrastructure and reliability are difficult to match at any price. AWS is so important to Amazon that the company chose AWS's CEO (Andy Jassy) to replace Jeff Bezos as Amazon's overall CEO.
AWS Products You Should Know
EC2 — Elastic Compute Cloud
Create virtual servers in the cloud instantly. Full control over OS and software. The workhorse of AWS — used to run websites, apps, and databases.
Lambda
Write a function in Python/Java, upload it, call it when needed. AWS handles all servers behind the scenes. "Serverless" — no server management required.
S3 — Simple Storage Solution
Infinitely scalable cloud storage. Think of it as a hard drive that never fills up. Used by movie studios, news orgs, and enterprises worldwide.
Redshift
A petabyte-scale data warehouse for analytics and business intelligence. Connects to S3 data and lets managers query massive datasets fast.
Workspaces
Virtual Windows PCs delivered through any browser or tablet. Great for call centers, remote workers, or anyone who needs a secure, managed desktop environment.
DynamoDB / Aurora
Database services. Aurora runs standard SQL databases. DynamoDB is a NoSQL option for high-volume, flexible data like social media apps.
Public vs. Private vs. Hybrid Cloud
Public cloud: Everything runs on a third-party vendor's shared infrastructure (AWS, Azure, Google). Cheapest, most scalable — but your data shares hardware with other customers.

Private cloud: Cloud computing technology (virtualization, elastic scaling) deployed on hardware the firm owns or leases exclusively. Bank of America built its own private cloud, estimating $2B in savings vs. using a public provider. The CIA runs a private cloud built with Amazon's technology — but only the CIA occupies it.

Hybrid cloud: A mix of both — core systems run privately in-house, but the firm can burst into the public cloud during demand spikes. IBM's "cloudbursting" service is a key example: your data center runs hot → seamlessly shifts overflow to IBM's public cloud → you pay only for what spills over, utility style.

Why not everyone goes public: Regulatory requirements (financial data, healthcare data), latency needs, legacy system complexity, or simple data sovereignty concerns may require private or hybrid approaches.

✓ Correct! This is cloud computing's killer feature for startups. With a self-owned data center, you'd need to have bought enough servers upfront for your worst-case scenario — most of which would sit idle 99% of the time.
