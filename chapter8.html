Chapter 08

Open Source Software

Free code, massive industry — why OSS reshapes every business decision in tech

📖
Vocabulary
Open Source Software (OSS)
Software that is free to use, and whose source code is openly published — meaning anyone can read it, modify it, and redistribute it, provided the modified version remains open and free. This is fundamentally different from proprietary commercial software, where the code is a closely guarded secret and customers pay per license. OSS is not just "free as in price" — it is "free as in freedom" (sometimes called FLOSS: Free/Libre/Open Source Software).
🧠 Analogy: A restaurant that not only gives away free food but also hands you the recipe — and asks that if you improve the recipe, you share your version with everyone too.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8; opensource.org/definition
Linux
The most important and widely adopted open source operating system, created in 1991 by then-21-year-old Finnish student Linus Torvalds, who gave it away for free. Today Linux powers ~92% of Amazon AWS cloud servers, over 40% of Microsoft Azure, the majority of the world's supercomputers, Android smartphones, TiVo, Mars rovers, and most of the web servers behind Google, Amazon, and Meta. It is maintained by thousands of paid contributors from companies including Google, IBM, Meta, and Microsoft.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8; Linux Foundation
Marginal Cost
The cost associated with producing one additional unit of a product. For physical goods (cars, shoes), each additional unit requires raw materials and labor. For software, the marginal cost of producing one more copy is effectively zero — duplicate the file and you're done. This is why category-leading software businesses are extraordinarily profitable: Microsoft generates over $5 billion per month from Windows and Office alone, with near-zero cost per additional customer.
🧠 Analogy: Writing a song costs time and money. But once it's written, streaming it to 1 person vs. 100 million costs virtually the same. Software has the same economics.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8; Investopedia.com/marginal-cost
Software as a Service (SaaS)
A form of cloud computing where users access software over the internet — typically through a web browser — rather than installing it locally. The vendor hosts and maintains the software on their own servers; users pay a subscription fee. Examples: Google Docs, Salesforce, Slack, Zoom. SaaS eliminates the need to buy, install, update, or manage software on individual machines.
🧠 Analogy: Instead of buying a car (installing software), you use Uber (SaaS) — pay only when you need it, no maintenance required, always the latest version.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8
Cloud Computing
Replacing computing resources — hardware or software — with services delivered over the internet. Rather than owning and maintaining physical servers, firms rent computing power, storage, and software from providers like Amazon (AWS), Microsoft (Azure), and Google (GCP). Cloud computing allows startups to access the same infrastructure as giant corporations, paying only for what they use.
🧠 Analogy: Cloud computing is like electricity from the grid. You don't build your own power plant — you plug in and pay for what you use. AWS is the power plant.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8
Virtualization
Software that allows a single physical computer (or cluster of computers) to function as multiple separate, independent machines — each running its own OS and software simultaneously. Virtualization is the core technology enabling most cloud computing. By running many "virtual machines" on one physical server, providers like AWS can use hardware far more efficiently and charge customers only for their slice of computing power.
🧠 Analogy: Virtualization is like converting one large house into multiple apartments. The building (hardware) is the same — but now it serves many independent tenants (virtual machines) simultaneously.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
An economic measure of the full cost of owning and using a product over its lifetime — not just the purchase price. For software, TCO includes the upfront license cost plus ongoing costs: training, IT support, maintenance, upgrades, and downtime. A product with a $0 price tag (like free OSS) can have a high TCO if it requires expensive expertise to configure, maintain, or troubleshoot. This is why "free" software isn't always cheaper in practice.
🧠 Analogy: A "free" puppy has a $0 sticker price. But food, vet bills, training, and boarding over 10 years make the true cost thousands of dollars. TCO captures the whole picture, not just the purchase price.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8; Gartner.com/TCO
LAMP Stack
An acronym for a widely used set of open source technologies used to build web applications: Linux (OS), Apache (web server), MySQL (database), and Python/Perl/PHP (programming language). All four components are free and open source. Facebook, YouTube, and countless other major websites were built on LAMP. It represents how OSS components can be assembled into a full production-grade technical infrastructure at near-zero software cost.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8
Scalability
The ability of a software system to handle increasing workloads — either by adding more resources or by redistributing work across many machines. A scalable system can grow from handling 100 users to 10 million users without requiring a complete rewrite. Linux is especially valued for scalability because the same OS runs on a $10 Raspberry Pi and on the world's fastest supercomputers, and can balance loads across thousands of machines simultaneously.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 8

Key Concepts
Who Actually Builds OSS — and Why?
The old image of OSS as lonely programmers coding for fun is largely outdated. Today, the majority of people working on open source projects are paid by commercially motivated employers. Companies like Google, IBM, Meta, Apple, and Microsoft pay engineers to contribute to Linux, Python, Android, and thousands of other OSS projects.

Why would a company contribute to free software? Several reasons:
• Shared infrastructure costs: Everyone needs a reliable OS. Splitting the development cost of Linux across thousands of companies is far cheaper than each building a proprietary OS alone — like splitting a pizza instead of everyone buying their own.
• Wedge strategy: Oracle gives Linux away free to undercut Microsoft. Every customer running Linux on Oracle hardware is a customer not buying Microsoft software.
• Network effects: More contributors = better, more secure software = more users = more contributors. OSS quality improves as its community grows.
• Talent and reputation: Firms that contribute to high-profile OSS projects attract top engineering talent.
Why Firms Choose OSS: The Six Benefits
💰 Cost
Barclays cut software costs by 90% switching to OSS. E*TRADE saved $13M/yr. Amazon cited OSS as key to $20M in tech savings.
🐛 Reliability
CMU's CyLab found Linux code 200x less buggy than commercial alternatives. More eyes on the code = fewer errors surviving undetected.
🔒 Security
Open code means vulnerabilities are found and patched faster. Government agencies value being able to audit the code themselves rather than trusting a vendor.
📈 Scalability
Linux runs on a $10 Raspberry Pi and a $500M supercomputer. E*TRADE's OSS infrastructure held up during record Fed-driven trading spikes when rivals' systems failed.
🔗 Interoperability
OSS products use open standards (like PDF) that work across any device, OS, or platform — no vendor lock-in.
⚡ Speed to Market
Motorola used OSS to accelerate phone development. The Zimbra team built their first email product in months using ~40 blocks of free, reusable OSS code.
"Given Enough Eyeballs, All Bugs Are Shallow"
This phrase — from open source pioneer Eric Raymond — means: the more developers who can read a program's source code, the more likely any error (bug) is to be spotted and fixed quickly. With proprietary software, only the company's own engineers can see the code. With OSS, potentially millions of developers worldwide are reading it constantly.

Why it matters for firms adopting OSS: Popular, widely-used OSS projects (Linux, Apache, Python) benefit from massive scrutiny and tend to be extremely reliable and secure. But the phrase also has a dangerous flip side — Heartbleed.

In 2014, a critical security flaw was found in OpenSSL — the encryption toolkit used by two-thirds of all websites. Despite being used in 30 billion annual e-commerce transactions, it was maintained by essentially two unpaid volunteers with almost no budget. The flaw had sat undetected for years. The lesson for managers: popularity alone does not guarantee that enough eyeballs are actually watching. Before adopting any OSS, audit who maintains it and how well-resourced that effort is.
How OSS Companies Actually Make Money
If the software is free, how do you build a billion-dollar business? Several proven models:
SupportRed Hat earns $3B/year selling subscriptions for software updates, patches, and enterprise support for Linux. The software is free; the expert help costs money.
Premium Add-onsMongoDB gives the core database away free but charges firms that run its management software on more than 8 servers — the free version gets you in the door, the premium features keep enterprises paying.
Hosted CloudDocker sells managed hosting so firms can use its OSS container technology without running it themselves. You use the free code, but pay them to manage it for you in the cloud.
Hardware SalesIBM gives away Linux support to pull customers toward IBM hardware and consulting services. Every dollar a customer saves on Microsoft software is a dollar available to spend on IBM.
Wedge StrategyOracle provides Linux for free — specifically to drive a wedge between Microsoft and its customers. Oracle and Microsoft compete in databases and enterprise software, so weakening Microsoft's OS grip benefits Oracle directly.
OSS, Hardware Sales, and Entrepreneurship
Impact on hardware sales: In the pre-Linux era, every major hardware vendor (IBM, HP, Sun, etc.) maintained its own proprietary, incompatible Unix variant. Each market was too small to attract much third-party software. Linux unified them into one massive standard — suddenly all hardware runs the same OS, software developers write once and reach everyone, and hardware firms can compete on performance and service rather than on OS lock-in.

Impact on startups and small firms: This is perhaps OSS's most democratizing effect. A startup in 2005 needed millions of dollars just to license server OS and database software before writing a single line of their actual product. Today, a two-person startup can download Linux, Apache, MySQL, Python, and dozens of other enterprise-grade tools for free — and deploy them on AWS (paying only for what they use). This has dramatically lowered the capital barrier to starting a tech company, enabling the explosion of startups we've seen over the past two decades.
The Risks of OSS: TCO and Beyond
Free software is not always cheap software. Managers must evaluate OSS honestly:
⚠️ High TCO
Complex OSS (like desktop Linux) may require expensive IT expertise to install, configure, and maintain — offsetting the zero license cost entirely.
⚠️ Support Uncertainty
Without a paid support contract, you're relying on a volunteer community that may not respond to your urgent issue on your timeline.
⚠️ Legal Exposure
Microsoft once claimed Linux violated 235 of its patents and collected licensing fees from Samsung, Fuji, and Xerox. OSS licenses (GPL, Apache, etc.) have varying legal obligations firms must track carefully.
⚠️ Abandonment Risk
Less popular OSS projects may lose their contributor community entirely. A product you've built your system on can become unmaintained overnight.

The Heartbleed lesson applies here too: just because something is widely used doesn't mean it's well-maintained. Always audit who supports the OSS you adopt.
