Chapter 10

Software Development

How software gets built, why projects fail, and what every manager needs to know

📖
Vocabulary
Programming Language
A formal set of rules, syntax, and instructions used to write software. Programmers write code in a language humans can read (like Python, Java, or C++), then that code is either compiled (translated all at once into machine code) or interpreted (translated line by line at runtime) so a processor can execute it. Popular languages: C++, Java, Python, Swift, JavaScript. The choice of language affects speed, portability, developer availability, and platform compatibility.
🧠 Analogy: A programming language is like a spoken language — French, Spanish, English. Each has its own grammar rules. A compiler is the translator that converts your French essay into machine-readable English before the processor can "read" it.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
High-Level vs. Low-Level Language
High-level languages (Python, Java, Swift) are closer to human language — readable, abstract, and easier to write but must be translated before a processor can run them. Low-level languages (Assembly, machine code) are closer to the binary instructions a processor actually executes — extremely fast and precise but nearly impossible for humans to read or write efficiently. Modern developers almost exclusively use high-level languages, relying on compilers to bridge the gap.
🧠 Analogy: High-level = ordering food in plain English ("I'd like a burger"). Low-level = giving the kitchen every exact instruction: "heat pan to 375°F, place patty on surface, wait 3 minutes 47 seconds..." One is much easier for humans; the other is what machines actually need.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC)
A structured process for planning, creating, testing, and deploying an information system. The SDLC defines six standard stages that all software development projects move through: Planning → Analysis → Design → Implementation → Testing → Maintenance. Following the SDLC helps teams catch problems early, reduce costs, and deploy more reliable systems. Errors found post-deployment can cost 100x more to fix than errors caught during design.
🧠 Analogy: The SDLC is like building a house — you don't just start hammering nails. You plan (blueprints), analyze (site survey), design (architect), build (contractors), inspect (testing), then maintain (repairs). Skipping steps creates expensive disasters.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Waterfall Methodology
A linear, sequential approach to software development where each phase must be fully completed before the next begins — like water flowing downward through stages: requirements → design → implementation → testing → deployment → maintenance. Benefits: clear documentation, defined requirements upfront, easy to manage. Drawbacks: inflexible, slow, and assumes you can fully define all requirements at the start — which is rarely realistic in fast-changing environments. Largely out of favor today.
🧠 Analogy: Waterfall is like ordering a custom birthday cake 6 months ahead and committing to every detail upfront. When your tastes change next month, you're stuck with what you ordered.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Agile Development
An iterative, flexible approach to software development where work is broken into small cycles (sprints), each producing a working piece of the final product. Teams continuously adapt to changing requirements, releasing improvements frequently rather than waiting for one big final delivery. Agile focuses on customer needs, rapid feedback, and constant improvement. It dominates modern software development because it handles the reality that requirements change as projects progress.
🧠 Analogy: Agile is like cooking a meal with the guest tasting as you go — adjusting seasoning and ingredients in real time. Waterfall is cooking everything based on a recipe decided months ago, serving it cold with no feedback until the end.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Scrum
The most popular framework for implementing agile development. Scrum organizes work into short cycles called sprints (1–6 weeks), uses small cross-functional teams (3–9 people), and defines clear roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Team), artifacts (product backlog, sprint backlog, task board), and ceremonies (sprint planning, daily standups, sprint reviews). The goal: build software faster, with more user input, and the ability to change direction quickly between sprints.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Product Owner
In scrum, the product owner represents the "voice of the customer" and the organization's business needs. They define and prioritize what features need to be built (the product backlog), write user stories that describe requirements from the user's perspective ("As a user, I need X so that Y"), and are ultimately accountable for ensuring the delivered product creates value. They are the bridge between business stakeholders and the development team.
🧠 Analogy: The product owner is like the client on a renovation show — they tell the team what they want, in what order, and judge whether the result meets their needs. They don't pick up a hammer, but they call the shots on what gets built.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Scope Creep (Feature Creep)
The gradual expansion of a project's scope beyond its original goals — usually through small, incremental additions that individually seem reasonable but collectively blow up timelines, budgets, and complexity. Scope creep is one of the most common causes of software project failure. It happens when requirements keep changing or growing mid-project without corresponding adjustments to time and budget. Clear upfront documentation and a disciplined product owner are the primary defenses against it.
🧠 Analogy: You hire a contractor to build a one-car garage. Midway through, you ask for a second bay, then a bathroom, then a loft apartment. Each request seems small — but together they triple the cost and delay completion by a year. That's scope creep.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)
The full economic cost of owning and operating an information system over its lifetime — not just the purchase price. TCO includes: software licenses or development costs, hardware, IT staff salaries, training, ongoing maintenance, security management, backup and disaster recovery, compliance auditing, and user support. Studies show firms spend 70–80% of their IS budget just to keep existing systems running. TCO is why "free" software isn't always cheap, and why cloud/SaaS can look attractive despite higher per-unit costs.
🧠 Analogy: Buying a car (sticker price) vs. owning a car (insurance, gas, maintenance, registration, repairs over 10 years). TCO captures the whole picture — the sticker price is just the beginning.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10; Gartner.com
Triple Constraint
The fundamental tension in any project between three competing constraints: Scope (what you're building), Time (how long you have), and Cost (what you can spend). You can only optimize two of the three — change one and it forces a tradeoff in the others. Want to add more features (scope)? Either pay more (cost) or take longer (time). Want to finish faster (time)? Either spend more or cut features. Every project manager must constantly navigate this triangle.
🧠 Analogy: "Good, fast, cheap — pick two." A fast, cheap renovation won't be high quality. A good, fast renovation won't be cheap. A good, cheap renovation won't be fast. The triple constraint is this same tradeoff applied to every software project.
Source: Project Management Institute (PMI)
Low Code / No Code (LCNC)
Highly visual software development tools that allow people to build information systems by dragging and dropping interface components, connecting logic blocks, and configuring workflows — with little to no traditional programming required. LCNC enables "citizen developers" (non-programmers) to build simple apps and automate tasks. Popular platforms: Airtable, Bubble, FlutterFlow, Microsoft Power Apps, Salesforce Lightning. Market size: ~$27B in 2023, growing 20% annually.
🧠 Analogy: LCNC is like building with Legos instead of carpentry. You assemble pre-built pieces into something functional without needing to know how to mill wood or cut joints. Great for simple structures — but you can't build a skyscraper out of Legos.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10
Citizen Developer
A non-IT employee who uses low code/no code tools to build applications or automate processes for their own department or work needs — without involvement from the formal IS organization. Citizen developers can accelerate innovation and reduce IT backlogs, especially in small firms without dedicated dev staff. However, they also introduce risks: poorly designed systems, data inconsistencies, security vulnerabilities, compliance gaps, and maintenance nightmares when the employee leaves.
Source: Gallaugher, Ch. 10; Gartner

Key Concepts
The Software Development Lifecycle — All 6 Stages
Every IS project — whether waterfall or agile — moves through these stages. The difference is in how linearly vs. iteratively they're approached.
1
Planning — Define the project's goals, scope, timeline, budget, and resources. Establish business case.
2
Analysis — Gather and document requirements. What does the system need to do? Who are the stakeholders?
3
Design — Define the architecture: which technologies, databases, interfaces, and integrations will be used?
4
Implementation — Developers write the actual code and build the system per the design specifications.
5
Testing — Verify the system works correctly across all hardware, use cases, and edge cases. Errors caught here are 100x cheaper to fix than post-deployment.
6
Maintenance — Ongoing support, security patches, user training, compliance auditing, backup, and updates after deployment.
Waterfall vs. Agile — Know the Difference Cold
This is a heavily tested concept. Know both the approach AND the tradeoffs of each.
🪣 Waterfall
  • Linear, sequential — one phase done before the next starts
  • All requirements defined upfront
  • Heavy documentation throughout
  • Customer sees product at the very end
  • Good for: stable, well-understood projects
  • Bad for: fast-changing requirements or industries
  • Largely out of favor — even the U.S. military moved away from it
⚡ Agile
  • Iterative — work in short sprints, deliver frequently
  • Requirements evolve as the project progresses
  • Continuous customer feedback and adjustment
  • Customer sees working software throughout
  • Good for: complex, evolving, or uncertain projects
  • Bad for: projects needing strict upfront contracts
  • Dominant methodology in modern tech firms
Scrum Roles, Artifacts & Ceremonies
Scrum is the most popular way to implement agile. Know these three categories:
Role
Product Owner
Voice of the customer. Defines and prioritizes the product backlog. Accountable for what gets built and why.
Role
Scrum Master
Runs meetings, keeps team on process, removes obstacles. Coaches the team — not a traditional project manager.
Role
Team
3–9 developers, designers, and testers. Self-organizing. Does the actual analysis, design, development, and testing.
Artifact
Product Backlog
Master list of all desired features written as user stories ("As a user, I need X so that Y"). Owned by the Product Owner.
Artifact
Sprint Backlog
The subset of backlog items committed to the current sprint — what the team will complete in the next 1–6 weeks.
Artifact
Task Board
Visual board tracking each task from "To Do" → "In Progress" → "Testing" → "Done." Keeps the team aligned daily.
Ceremony
Sprint Planning
Team decides what stories to tackle in the upcoming sprint and how to approach them.
Ceremony
Daily Standup
≤15-min standing meeting: What did I do yesterday? What am I doing today? What's blocking me? Keeps everyone in sync.
Ceremony
Sprint Review & Retro
Demo completed work to stakeholders. Then discuss what went well and what to improve for next sprint.
Why Technology Projects Fail — And How to Prevent It
1 in 3 large technology projects fails. The yearly cost of failed software in the U.S. alone is estimated at $60–70 billion. Common failure causes:
✗No clear authority or leadership — no one owns the outcome (the #1 HealthCare.gov problem: no one knew who was in charge)
✗Technology teams excluded from planning meetings — people making decisions don't understand the tech
✗Minimal or no testing — HealthCare.gov launched nationally to 250,000 users with zero large-scale testing; only 8 people enrolled day one
✗Scope creep — requirements keep expanding mid-project without adjusting time and budget
✗Poor communication across stakeholders — developers, users, managers, and executives not in sync
✗Unrealistic timelines and budgets — pressure to cut corners leads to inadequate testing and fragile systems
✗Ignoring people and procedures — IS has 5 components (hardware, software, data, people, procedures). Failing any one sinks the project

Prevention: Strong executive sponsorship, clear ownership and authority, incremental rollouts ("open in small concentric circles, then expand"), rigorous testing before deployment, cross-functional communication via daily standups, and following SDLC/agile best practices.
The Make, Buy, or Rent Decision
When a firm needs software, it has five options: build it in-house, buy packaged software, use OSS, use SaaS/cloud, or outsource development. Most firms use a mix. Key factors that determine the right choice:
🏆 Competitive Advantage
If a function creates unique competitive advantage (like Amazon's recommendation engine), build it yourself — don't outsource your secret sauce.
🔒 Security
Can you trust a vendor with your code, data, and business processes? Are there audit provisions? Non-compete clauses for vendor staff?
⚖️ Legal & Compliance
Does your industry have regulations (HIPAA, SEC Rule 17a-4) that constrain which technologies you can use or where data can be stored?
👥 Skill & Labor
Do you have the in-house expertise to build and maintain it? Even if skilled, can you afford to pull them off other projects?
💰 Cost (TCO)
Evaluate all costs over the system's lifetime: development, licensing, maintenance, training, security, support — not just the upfront price.
⏱️ Time
Do you have enough time to build, test, and deploy? Sometimes buying or renting is the only realistic option given deadlines.
LCNC: Benefits and Risks Managers Must Know
Low code/no code tools are powerful for the right use cases — but dangerous when misused. Know both sides cold for the test:

When LCNC works well: Simple internal tools, rapid prototyping, small firms without IS staff, quick front-end mockups for developers to build on, time-sensitive deployments.

The risks managers must watch for:
• Data integrity: A citizen developer might collect duplicate or inconsistent data without knowing it
• Compliance gaps: Non-IT staff may not know HIPAA, FERPA, or SEC regulations that govern how data can be stored and used
• Scalability failures: A system built for 5 users often breaks when 500 use it simultaneously
• Maintenance orphans: The citizen developer leaves the company — nobody can update or fix the system they built
• Vendor risk: LCNC startup tools come and go. If the vendor shuts down, your system is stranded
• Security holes: Citizen developers rarely think about what data could be stolen, corrupted, or exposed

Key rule: No citizen developer should ever directly create or modify data in a firm's critical production systems without IS oversight.

Question 1
A government agency is building a large public-facing website. Halfway through a 2-year waterfall project, new legislation changes several core requirements. The agency must now rebuild large portions of work already completed, causing massive delays and cost overruns. What does this situation most directly illustrate as a weakness of waterfall?
A
Waterfall is too expensive because it requires too many developers working simultaneously.
B
Waterfall's rigid sequential structure assumes requirements are fully knowable upfront — when they change mid-project, the methodology provides no mechanism to adapt without costly rework.
C
The problem is agile's fault — the agency should have used waterfall's daily standup meetings to catch the issue earlier.
D
Waterfall failed here because it lacks a product backlog to handle changing priorities.
✓ Correct! Waterfall's biggest weakness is exactly this — it bets everything on requirements being perfectly defined at the start and locked in forever. Real-world projects rarely work that way. Agile solves this by expecting requirements to change, building in regular feedback loops, and delivering working software incrementally so changes can be incorporated without scrapping months of work.
✗ Not quite. The core waterfall problem here is rigidity — it flows in one direction (like a waterfall) and treats requirements as immutable. When reality changes (new legislation, new user needs, new business priorities), waterfall has no built-in mechanism to adapt. Agile was specifically designed to solve this problem through iterative sprints and continuous feedback.
Question 2
In a scrum team, who is responsible for prioritizing which features get built in the next sprint, and why is that role critical to project success?
A
The Scrum Master, because they control the team's schedule and workflow.
B
The development team, because they best understand what's technically feasible to build next.
C
The Product Owner, because they represent the customer's and organization's needs — ensuring the team always works on the highest-value features, not just the most technically interesting ones.
D
The IT Director, because someone with executive authority must approve all sprint priorities.
✓ Correct! The Product Owner is the bridge between business value and technical execution. Without someone continuously prioritizing the backlog based on customer and organizational needs, development teams risk building technically impressive features that nobody actually wanted. The Product Owner prevents this by keeping the most valuable work at the top of the sprint backlog at all times.
✗ Not quite. The Scrum Master runs meetings and removes obstacles — they don't own priorities. The development team decides HOW to build things, not WHAT to build next. The Product Owner owns the product backlog and is specifically responsible for representing customer value and organizational priorities in every sprint planning meeting.
Question 3
HealthCare.gov launched nationally to 250,000 users on day one. Only 8 people successfully enrolled. Former Twitter CTO Mike Abbott said, "You never open a service like this to everyone at once. You open it in small concentric circles." Which project management failure does Abbott's advice most directly address?
A
The site used the wrong programming language, causing poor performance under load.
B
The site's user interface violated accessibility standards required by federal law.
C
Inadequate testing and a "big bang" deployment strategy — launching to everyone simultaneously with no incremental rollout meant undetected flaws hit millions of users instantly, with no ability to catch and fix issues before full-scale damage occurred.
D
The project suffered from scope creep — too many features were added before launch.
✓ Correct! Abbott's advice is about incremental rollout — deploy to one state, fix issues, then expand. HealthCare.gov skipped all of this. Studies show errors found post-deployment cost 100x more to fix than errors caught before. Launching to the entire country simultaneously turned every uncaught bug into a national crisis. The "small concentric circles" approach lets you catch and fix problems while they're still manageable.
✗ Not quite. The issue Abbott is addressing is the "big bang" launch strategy combined with inadequate testing. The site was never tested at anything close to national scale before going live. His advice — incremental rollout — is about catching and fixing bugs at small scale before exposing every user to them simultaneously. This is one of the most fundamental project management lessons from HealthCare.gov.
Question 4
A nonprofit with no IT staff uses a no-code platform to build a donor management system. The volunteer who built it quits. A year later, the platform vendor goes out of business. The nonprofit is left with an unusable system and no one who understands it. Which risks of LCNC/citizen development does this scenario illustrate?
A
Security vulnerabilities and data compliance gaps from an untrained developer.
B
Scalability failure — LCNC systems can't handle more than 10 concurrent users.
C
Maintenance orphan risk (the developer left and no one can maintain the system) and vendor risk (the LCNC platform shut down, stranding the system with no migration path).
D
This scenario is fine — LCNC tools are specifically designed for nonprofits with no IT staff.
✓ Correct! This scenario hits two of the most important LCNC risks: (1) the "maintenance orphan" — when the person who built the system leaves, nobody can support or update it; and (2) vendor risk — LCNC is a young, fragmented market full of startups, and if the vendor shuts down, your system may become completely unusable with no way to export or migrate your data. Both risks must be evaluated before choosing LCNC for anything critical.
✗ Not quite. While security and scalability are real LCNC risks, this specific scenario illustrates maintenance orphan risk (nobody left who can maintain the system after the builder leaves) and vendor risk (the LCNC startup went out of business). These are two of the most commonly overlooked LCNC dangers — especially in nonprofits and small organizations that don't have IS staff to pick up the pieces.
Question 5
Amazon, Netflix, and Dell all initially considered using third-party software for recommendation engines and inventory fulfillment — functions that directly shape the customer experience and drive revenue. All three ultimately chose to build these systems in-house. Which factor from the make/buy/rent framework best explains this decision?
A
Cost — building in-house is almost always cheaper than buying packaged software.
B
Time — packaged software would have taken longer to deploy than custom development.
C
Competitive advantage — these functions were too central to each firm's differentiation to outsource. If a competitor can buy the same software, it eliminates any advantage. Proprietary systems that competitors can't easily replicate are a source of sustained competitive advantage.
D
Legal compliance — industry regulations prohibited these firms from using third-party recommendation software.
✓ Correct! This is the most important principle in the make/buy/rent decision: if a function creates vital, differentiating competitive advantage — don't outsource it. Amazon's recommendation engine is a core reason customers buy more. Netflix's algorithm is a core reason subscribers stay. If either used third-party software, any competitor could buy the same system and immediately match them. Proprietary, in-house systems that competitors can't replicate are one of the strongest forms of sustained competitive advantage.
✗ Not quite. Custom in-house development is usually more expensive and slower than buying packaged software. The reason all three firms chose to build was competitive advantage. When a function is core to why customers choose you over competitors, outsourcing it means a competitor can simply buy the same software and match you instantly. You never outsource your secret sauce.
